## 4. Institutional AI Maturity Model

Proposed five-level model for scoring each university in the dataset, adapted from SEI, MITRE, and sector-specific research:

### Level 1: Exploratory
- **Governance:** No formal AI policy; ad hoc faculty decisions
- **Teaching:** Individual faculty experiments; no institutional guidance
- **Research:** No dedicated AI computing resources or centers
- **Operations:** No AI in administrative processes
- **Workforce:** No AI training programs for faculty or staff
- **Signal phrases:** "exploring," "considering," "pilot," "working group," "no formal policy"

### Level 2: Reactive
- **Governance:** Basic acceptable use policy exists; reactive to incidents
- **Teaching:** Syllabus-level policies emerging; some faculty workshops
- **Research:** Limited AI tools available; no dedicated infrastructure
- **Operations:** Individual AI tool use (e.g., ChatGPT for drafting)
- **Workforce:** One-off AI workshops or seminars
- **Signal phrases:** "guidelines issued," "policy released," "initial training," "first workshop"

### Level 3: Strategic
- **Governance:** Formal AI strategy with dedicated leadership (AI council, CIO/CAO oversight)
- **Teaching:** AI literacy integrated into curriculum; faculty development programs running
- **Research:** Dedicated AI computing resources; at least one AI-focused center
- **Operations:** AI deployed in at least 2-3 administrative areas (chatbots, enrollment, advising)
- **Workforce:** Structured professional development with ongoing programs
- **Signal phrases:** "AI strategy," "institutional framework," "AI council," "campus-wide," "center launch"

### Level 4: Integrated
- **Governance:** Comprehensive governance framework with risk management; regular review cycles
- **Teaching:** AI embedded across most programs; assessment redesigned for AI era; outcome measurement
- **Research:** Major computing infrastructure (GPU clusters); multiple AI centers; industry partnerships
- **Operations:** AI integrated into core workflows; data systems unified; measurable efficiency gains
- **Workforce:** AI competency requirements for new hires; ongoing certification programs
- **Signal phrases:** "enterprise deployment," "institution-wide integration," "AI-driven decision making," "data-unified"

### Level 5: Transformative
- **Governance:** Adaptive governance that evolves with technology; contributing to sector standards
- **Teaching:** AI-native pedagogical models; personalized learning at scale; continuous innovation
- **Research:** Leading AI research output; shaping national/international AI agenda; open-source contributions
- **Operations:** AI-first operations; predictive institutional management; full data interoperability
- **Workforce:** AI workforce hub for region; industry certification authority; talent exporter
- **Signal phrases:** "AI-native," "sector leader," "national model," "transformative," "AI-first"

### Assessment Dimensions (per level):

Each university can be scored 1-5 across eight dimensions:

1. **Strategy & Leadership** -- Does institution have an articulated AI vision and dedicated leadership?
2. **Policy & Governance** -- Are there formal, comprehensive AI policies covering ethics, use, and risk?
3. **Teaching & Curriculum** -- How deeply is AI integrated into pedagogy and degree programs?
4. **Research Infrastructure** -- What dedicated AI computing and research capacity exists?
5. **Student Services & Experience** -- Are AI-powered tools deployed for the student lifecycle?
6. **Workforce & Community** -- Does the institution serve as an AI workforce development hub?
7. **Partnerships & Ecosystem** -- How extensive are industry, cross-institution, and international AI partnerships?
8. **Equity & Inclusion** -- Are there active measures to ensure equitable AI access and address bias?

## 6. Key Tensions & Debates to Track

These are the fault lines that generate the most interesting analysis and commentary:

1. **Academic integrity vs. AI integration** -- 88% of students use GenAI for assessments, but institutions are still debating acceptable use boundaries. The gap between policy and practice is widening.

2. **Speed of adoption vs. equity** -- Well-resourced R1s are deploying enterprise AI platforms while community colleges and Global South institutions lack basic infrastructure. AI may widen existing inequalities.

3. **Institutional control vs. faculty autonomy** -- Top-down AI strategies conflict with traditions of academic freedom. 343-university study found no consistency in approach.

4. **Innovation vs. regulatory compliance** -- EU AI Act compliance deadlines (August 2026) constrain experimental use of AI in admissions, assessment, and student monitoring.

5. **AI as tool vs. AI as transformation** -- Is AI just another EdTech tool, or does it fundamentally change what universities are and do? Deloitte and BCG reports take the transformative view.

6. **Human skills vs. technical skills** -- Liberal arts advocates argue human skills become MORE valuable in the AI era; STEM advocates argue AI literacy is the new baseline. Both may be right.

7. **Open vs. proprietary platforms** -- Universities choosing between Google/Microsoft ecosystems and open-source alternatives face lock-in vs. capability tradeoffs.

8. **National sovereignty vs. global collaboration** -- Countries pursuing sovereign AI (South Korea, China, Gulf states) while universities pursue international research partnerships.

9. **Student as consumer vs. student as learner** -- AI-powered convenience tools may optimize for satisfaction metrics over genuine learning outcomes.

10. **Short-term ROI vs. long-term institutional health** -- Pressure to show immediate AI returns vs. building sustainable AI capacity and culture.

## 8. Report Structure Recommendations

When generating automated reports from this taxonomy, prioritize:

1. **Lead with tensions and what's changing**, not just what happened. The most valuable analysis surfaces emerging patterns and conflicts.

2. **Segment by institution type and region** to reveal uneven adoption. A single "higher ed" view misses the most important dynamics.

3. **Track velocity, not just position**. A community college moving from Level 1 to Level 2 may be more significant than an R1 maintaining Level 4.

4. **Surface the policy-practice gap** repeatedly. The data shows a persistent disconnect between what institutions say and what happens in classrooms.

5. **Connect dots across categories**. The most interesting stories are at intersections: a governance decision that enables a teaching innovation, or an equity gap that undermines an enterprise deployment.

6. **Use the maturity model as a scoring framework** for longitudinal tracking across the dataset.
